01.

Chemical basis of insect adaptation to plant toxin


Human inability to feed unprocessed cassava, poisoning due to extended feeding of grass pea, dermatitis when exposed to carrot grass and the enigma of litchi toxicity -- these are the cases that remind us about what a plant toxin is.

Defending themselves from herbivores, plants produce and utilize toxins (chemical weapon) to restrict herbivore digestive capability or target their physiological processes. At the same time, interestingly, there is no plant species which is not fed upon by an herbivore.

I am exploring an unexamined adaptation of a specialized insect herbivore Zygogramma bicolorata to the phytotoxin of an obnoxious weed plant Parthenium hysterophorus.

02.

Evolution of small founding population


A small founding population is observed when a handful of interbreeding individuals gets dispersed/transported from the parent population and becomes the founding members in establishing a new isolated population.

Small founding population is characterized by reduced standing genetic variation, and is prone to inbreeding, demographic stochasticity and genetic drift.

I am starting with the genomic scan of the beetle (Zygogramma bicolorata) population in India to understand the role of habitat heterogeneity and local environmental stressors in improving the population-level genomic variability.
